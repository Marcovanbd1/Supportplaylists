How it works

From what someone says, to a Support Playlist

Support Playlists sit between daily life and clinical care. They do not diagnose. They help people see a path.

Step 1
Listen to the real story
Someone types or speaks in their own words. No medical language needed.

The system listens for patterns. Mood. Safety. Money. Caring. Housing. Connection.
Step 2
Curate, do not overwhelm
Instead of a long list of links, the person receives a short playlist. Immediate options. Local options. Follow up.

Always grounded in real services. Local, national, digital.
Step 3
Share and return
Each playlist can be saved, shared, and updated. With a friend. A family member. A support worker.

It becomes a living plan rather than a single leaflet.
Examples

Designed for the situations we meet every week

Support Playlists can sit inside the NHS App, practice websites, community hubs, or local campaigns.

Scenario
Feeling low or anxious
A Support Playlist might include:
Safe urgent support if you feel at risk Local talking therapy or mental health support Everyday steps you can try yourself When to contact your GP team
People do not need to know the right clinical term. They just need somewhere to start.
  • Respectful language that does not label the person. Focused on what might help today and over the next few weeks.
  • Makes clear when to seek urgent help, and when a booked appointment is needed.
Scenario
Leaving hospital and going home
A Support Playlist might include:
Who to phone if you are worried tonight Local voluntary support and check in services Help with shopping, money or equipment When to ask for more help from your GP or hospital team
Recovery is not only clinical. People also need food, friendship, and confidence.
  • Support Playlists give carers and family a clear picture of how to help.
  • They can be shared in a ward, in a GP practice, or by a volunteer on the doorstep.
For NHS and local partners

A new layer for smart, human triage

Support Playlists do not replace clinical systems. They sit alongside them, making it easier for people to reach the right kind of help earlier.

Where Support Playlists can sit.
  • Inside the NHS App, as a way to turn a typed concern into a Support Playlist.
  • On primary care and PCN websites, as a soft front door to non clinical support.
  • As QR codes in waiting rooms, libraries, schools, workplaces, and community venues.

What this can help with.
  • Shaping demand before it reaches clinical triage. Some needs are better met by community, money, housing or emotional support.
  • Giving staff a simple, trusted thing to send people. One link, many options.
Safety and design
Built for emotional and digital safety
Support Playlists are being developed with:
  • • Clear guard rails around clinical advice and risk.
  • • Local curation, so partners can shape what appears in each area.
  • • Plain language that fits a wide range of reading ages.
  • • A focus on emotional tone. People should feel understood, not judged.
