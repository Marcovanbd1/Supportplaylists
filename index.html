What Support Playlists are

One playlist. The most relevant support.

A Support Playlist brings together the most relevant local, national and digital options in one place. It responds to how a person describes what is going on in their own words.

It is not a search engine. It is not a symptom checker. It is not another app to download. It is a calm guide into the right kind of help.

  • feeling low, stressed or overwhelmed
  • loneliness or disconnection
  • worries about money, food or housing
  • family pressures or relationship strain
  • grief, change or uncertainty

The space around care

Before care. After care. Often missed.

Most of the difficulty happens just before and just after clinical care.

There is a moment before, when someone is trying to understand what is happening and decide what to do next. And a moment after, when they are trying to recover, adjust or get back on their feet.

These moments sit outside formal clinical pathways. They are emotional, practical and social. Support Playlists live in this space around care. They hold the entry moment and the recovery moment, surrounding clinical care with context, continuity and connection.

The moments in between

Support Playlists notice the space before care and after care, when people are often most unsure, most alone and most likely to fall through the gaps.

Entry moments People are unsure what to do next. Support Playlists provide context and continuity.
Clinical care Clinical pathways, decisions and treatment sit at the centre.
Recovery moments Daily life resumes. Risk of isolation or relapse is highest. Support Playlists hold this moment.
Why this matters

Reducing friction. Preventing gaps.

Many people fall into the gaps between community life and clinical care. Support may exist, but it is scattered, hard to see or difficult to navigate.

The emotional and cognitive effort needed to find help often stops people even trying.

Support Playlists reduce that friction. They:

  • help people move with more confidence
  • support resilience, early intervention and prevention
  • give context and practical support around clinical care
  • make better use of what already exists in neighbourhoods
Support Playlists introduce a new layer around existing services. They do not replace clinical care or assess symptoms.
